## 1. Notes on Scopes and Limitations

Before responding to individual Tasks, it is worth noting that NCF-FS 2022 provides broad developmental competencies and learning outcomes rather than detailed prescriptions for content design. As a result, many of the recommendations below are based on a combination of NCF-FS guidance, early childhood education practice, and developmental research rather than direct curriculum mandate. Where a recommendation is drawn primarily from developmental research rather than an explicit NCF-FS competency, I have indicated this in the rationale.

A second consideration is the variability that exists within the proposed age bands. In practice, children between 3 and 6 years old show substantial difference in language exposure, socio-emotional development, numeracy experiences and familiarity with English or other instructional languages. The band-wise progressions suggested below should therefore be treated as developmental ranges intended to guide content design rather than as fixed expectations that all children will meet at a particular age. In a digital product context, it may be useful to validate some of these assumptions through pilot testing and gameplay observation before finalizing progression decisions.

## Task 1 — Vocabulary Pools

**Brief Rationale:** G5 is designed to support vocabulary development, categorization, and early phonological awareness. Vocabulary progression must move from highly familiar and concrete words in Band 1 to richer descriptive vocabulary and sound awareness activities in Bands 3 & 4 (simple to complex, known to unknown). Wherever possible, vocabulary should reflect the experiences of children growing up in Indian homes, pre-schools, and neighborhoods.

**Language of Delivery:** For the current version of the product, I would recommend a bilingual English–Malayalam model across all four bands. Children in the 3–6 age range often demonstrate stronger comprehension in their home language, while gradual exposure to English supports vocabulary development and school readiness. Key instructions, target vocabulary, and feedback can be presented in both languages, with the option for parents to select English-only delivery in future versions.

### Band 1 (Age 3–4 Years)

**Developmental Focus:** Children in this stage are primarily building receptive vocabulary and establishing word-object associations. Vocabulary should be highly concrete, visually recognizable, and drawn from day-to-day life. Verbs should represent actions that can be demonstrated physically.

**1. Simple Nouns**

Ball, Cup, Book, Bag, Shoe, Hat, Spoon, Plate, Banana, Mango, Apple, Flower, Tree, Leaf, Fish, Frog, Cow, Dog, Cat, Bus, Car, Kite, Water, Moon, Star

**2. Action Verbs**

Run, Jump, Hop, Sit, Eat, Drink, Clap, Wave, Walk, Kick, Hug, Sleep, Wash, Draw, Look, Push, Pull, Throw, Catch, Smile

**3. Descriptors**

1. Colors: Red, Blue, Green, Yellow
2. Size: Big, Small
3. Shapes: Round, Square
4. Sense: Hot, Cool

**Suggested categories**

- Animals
- Foods
- Things at Home
- Nature
- Vehicles

### Band 2 (Age 4–5 Years)

**Developmental Focus:** Children begin to organize vocabulary into categories and understand attributes more systematically. They can work with slightly less familiar vocabulary and are increasingly able to connect words with actions and functions.

**1. Simple Nouns**

Pencil, Eraser, Bottle, Basket, Coconut, Papaya, Auto, Train, River, Pond, Nest, Feather, Butterfly, Duck, Goat, Hen, Drum, Ladder, Umbrella, Soap, Towel, Garden, Seed, Pot

**2. Action Verbs**

Dance, Color, Gather, Carry, Plant, Water, Brush, Open, Close, Share, Build, Sweep, Bounce, Crawl, Spin, Fold, Stack, Point, Search

**3. Descriptors**

1. Color: Orange, Purple, Pink, Brown (Secondary)
2. Shapes: Triangle, Rectangle
3. Size and Positions: Tall, Short, Long, Near, Far
4. Texture: Soft, Hard

**Suggested categories**

- Animals and their categories
- Fruits and vegetables
- Household objects
- School objects
- Nature objects

### Band 3 (Age 4.5–5.5 Years)

**Developmental Focus:** Children begin developing phonological awareness and can work with more complex categorization tasks. Vocabulary can include descriptive and relational concepts. Sound-based activities can begin at this level.

**1. Simple Nouns**

Farmer, Doctor, Teacher, Market, Temple, Bridge, Forest, Village, Basket, Peacock, Tiger, Squirrel, Turtle, Tadpole, Rainbow, Mountain, Bucket, Necklace, Blanket, Festival, Garden, Parrot, Riverbank, Beehive, Pathway

**2. Action Verbs**

Follow, Listen, Gather, Balance, Measure, Whisper, Sprinkle, Arrange, Compare, Discover, Protect, Rescue, Observe, Explain, Collect, Deliver, Decorate, Identify

**3. Descriptors**

Heavy, Light, Thick, Thin, Empty, Full, Smooth, Rough, Fast, Slow, Early, Late

**4. Phoneme awareness preparation**

- Cat – Hat
- Sun – Fun
- Bell – Well
- Log – Frog
- Pin – Tin

At this stage the focus must remain playful and auditory rather than explicitly instructional.

### Band 4 (Age 5.5–6 Years)

**Developmental Focus:** Children can playfully engage with beginning phoneme manipulation, minimal pair discrimination, and more sophisticated categorization. Vocabulary can support emergent literacy while remaining grounded in oral language.

**1. Simple Nouns**

Carpenter, Musician, Painter, Journey, Direction, Pattern, Clock, Meadow, Footprint, Community, Celebration, Instrument, Orchestra, Village fair, Seedling, Workshop, Sculpture, Backpack, Adventure, Message, Puzzle, Toolbox, Flower bouquet

**2. Action Verbs**

Compare, Imagine, Describe, Organize, Investigate, Construct, Predict, Remember, Observe, Explain, Recognize, Create, Decorate, Discover, Complete, Measure, Arrange, Explore, Identify

**3. Descriptors**

Narrow, Wide, Deep, Shallow, Curved, Straight, Bright, Dim, Equal, Different, Similar, Opposite

**4. Minimal pairs (phoneme awareness)**

- Pin – Bin
- Ten – Den
- Coat – Goat
- Fan – Van
- Cap – Gap
- Bat – Pat
- Sip – Zip
- Rope – Road
- Rice – Rise
- Tuck – Duck

These should be presented orally with strong visual support and should not require formal decoding or reading skills.

### Additional Suggestions

1. Vocabulary should be recycled across multiple contexts rather than introduced only once. Repeated exposure is more likely to support retention than continual introduction of new words.
2. Wherever possible, nouns and verbs should be represented through familiar visual contexts from Indian homes, schools, neighborhoods, and natural environments.
3. Phonological awareness tasks should focus on listening and sound discrimination rather than letter-sound correspondence. Formal phonics instruction appears beyond the scope of the proposed age range and should be introduced cautiously.
4. For long-term content production, I would recommend developing a master vocabulary bank substantially larger than the minimum required for gameplay, allowing content rotation and reducing repetition for returning users.

## Task 2 — Emotion Sets (R2 Feeling Pond)

**Rationale:** R2 functions as both an emotional check-in and a session readiness activity. At younger ages, children are typically able to recognize and label a small set of basic emotions. As they grow older, they become increasingly able to connect emotions with causes, understand that different people may feel differently in the same situations, and talk about their own feelings using simple language. The progression below moves from emotion recognition (B1) to cause-and-effect understanding (B2–B3) and perspective taking in B4.

### Band 1 (Age 3–4 Years)

At this stage I would recommend focusing only on basic emotions that can be easily recognized through facial expressions and everyday situations.

**1. Emotion Vocabulary:** Happy, Sad, Angry, Scared

**2. Example Situations**

- Happy: playing with a friend
- Sad: a toy breaks
- Angry: unable to open a box or reach a toy
- Scared: hearing thunder or a loud sound

### Band 2 (Age 4–5 Years)

Children begin to understand that feelings happen for a reason and can talk about simple causes.

**1. Emotion Vocabulary**

Happy, Sad, Angry, Scared, Excited, Surprised, Tired, Calm

**2. Cause-and-effect sentence stems**

- I feel happy because ________
- I feel sad because ________
- I feel excited because ________
- I feel angry because ________

**Example responses**

- I feel happy because I got chocolate
- I feel sad because my balloon burst
- I feel excited because we are going to the park

### Band 3 (Age 4.5–5.5 Years)

Children can begin discussing more complex emotions and recognizing that feelings may differ across situations.

**1. Emotion Vocabulary**

Happy, Sad, Angry, Scared, Excited, Calm, Proud, Worried, Shy, Disappointed

**2. Cause-and-effect sentences**

- I feel _____ because _____ happened
- I feel _____ when _____
- I felt _____ but then _____

**3. Example responses**

- I feel proud because I finished drawing
- I feel worried because I cannot find my dog

**Perspective-taking scenarios**

1. Aadil wins a race and feels happy. His friend did not win and feels sad.
2. Kaashi gets a new paintbrush and feels excited. Another character does not like painting and feels uninterested.
3. Rayan is happy because it is raining. Another character is sad because outdoor play is cancelled.

### Band 4 (Age 5.5–6 Years)

Children can usually recognize that people may experience different emotions in the same situation and can explain simple reasons for those differences.

**1. Emotion Vocabulary**

Happy, Sad, Angry, Scared, Excited, Calm, Proud, Worried, Shy, Disappointed, Frustrated, Nervous, Lonely, Grateful

**2. Cause-and-effect sentences**

- I feel ______ because ________
- I think ________ feels _________ because ________
- We both saw the same thing, but I feel _______ and _______ feels ______

**3. Perspective-taking scenarios**

- Grandpa Frog is happy that school is starting. A younger frog feels nervous about the first day.
- Mama Fish shares food equally. One fish feels grateful, while another still wants more.
- Rayan finishes his picture and feels proud; a friend compares their own picture and feels disappointed.
- Viyan enjoys trying something new. Another character feels worried because it looks difficult.
- Baby fish moves to a new pond and feels excited. Another fish feels lonely because his friend left.

### Cultural Considerations

I would avoid introducing emotions such as guilt, shame, jealousy, humiliation, or betrayal as target vocabulary at the preschool stage. These emotions often require more advanced social and moral understanding and may be difficult for children in the age 3–6 range to interpret consistently.

The scenarios above are grounded in everyday preschool experiences such as play, sharing, friendship, family interactions, school routines, and simple successes or disappointments. These are likely to be more meaningful and relatable for children than abstract or highly dramatic situations.

## Task 3 — Number Ranges and Operation Scope (G6, G8, G9, G10)

**Rationale:** The proposed progression is broadly aligned with developmental expectations outlined in NCF-FS. Across the foundational years, children move from perceptual quantity recognition and one-to-one correspondence towards counting, simple operations and fair sharing situations. Mathematical experience at this age level should remain concrete and action-based, with children working primarily through objects, pictures, movements, and real-world situations before being expected to work with abstract symbols.

### G6 — Marble Compare

**Current Assumption**

- B1: Quantity discrimination up to 5
- B2: Quantity discrimination up to 10

**Recommendation**

I would retain this progression.

For Band 1, the emphasis should be on visually recognizing "more", "less", and "same" without requiring formal counting. Children in this age range often identify quantities up to 3 or 4 perceptually (subitizing) and can compare small groups when the difference is obvious. By Band 2, extending comparison tasks up to 10 is appropriate, provided children can still rely on visual comparison and simple counting strategies where needed.

### G8 — Lily Pad Count

**Current Assumption**

- B2: Counting to 5
- B3: Counting to 10
- B3 Hard: Backward counting

**Recommendation**

I would broadly support this progression. Band 2 should focus on stable counting sequences and one-to-one correspondence while counting sets up to 5. Band 3 can extend counting to 10, with increasing emphasis on accuracy and tracking each object counted. Backward counting may be introduced as a challenge level in Band 3, but should begin with very small ranges (e.g., 5 to 1) before progressing further.

### G9 — Lily Pad Maths

**Current Assumption**

- B3: Addition to 5
- B3 Hard: Subtraction to 5
- B4: Addition and subtraction up to 20

**Recommendation**

I would like to suggest a slightly more gradual progression here. For Band 3, addition and subtraction should remain within 5 and should be presented entirely through concrete objects and visual supports. For Band 4, children can begin working with larger quantities, but operations within 10 are likely to be more developmentally secure before extending towards 20. We can include operations up to 20, but they should remain heavily supported by visual representations rather than relying on mental calculation.

The focus at this stage should be understanding quantity relationships rather than achieving computational speed or accuracy with large numbers.

### G10 — Fish Portions

**Current Assumption**

- B2: Sharing between 2
- B3: Sharing between 3–4
- B4: Half, third, quarter fractions

**Recommendation**

I would retain this progression with one change/consideration. The primary learning goal should remain fairness and equal sharing rather than formal fraction instruction. For Band 2, children can share objects equally between two characters. For Band 3, sharing can extend to three and four characters using concrete visual examples.

For Band 4, terms such as "half", "third", and "quarter" may be introduced, but always within meaningful sharing situations. The concepts should emerge from the act of sharing rather than from formal fraction notation.

### Introduction of Written Numerals

**Recommendation**

I would recommend introducing written numerals alongside pictorial representations from Band 2 onwards. Band 1 should primarily use objects, pictures, and quantity experiences without requiring numeral recognition. From Band 2, children can begin seeing numerals alongside counted objects (e.g., five fish accompanied by the numeral 5). At this stage, numerals function as familiar visual symbols rather than as abstract mathematical notation. By Band 3, numerals can appear consistently across counting activities. By Band 4, children can be expected to use numerals as meaningful representations of quantity in counting and simple operations.

| Band | Numeral Exposure |
|------|------------------|
| B1 | Objects and quantities only |
| B2 | Objects with numerals 1–5 |
| B3 | Numerals used consistently alongside quantities |
| B4 | Numerals used within counting and operations |

In my view, introducing numerals only at Band 3 would be unnecessarily late, particularly given children's frequent exposure to numbers in homes, schools, books, calendars, vehicles, and everyday environments.
